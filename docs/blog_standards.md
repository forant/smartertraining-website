# SmarterTraining Blog Editorial Standards

## Purpose

The SmarterTraining blog exists to help cyclists train intelligently within the constraints of real life.

The blog is not:
- an SEO content farm
- a generic fitness publication
- a hype-driven AI blog
- a pseudo-scientific performance site
- an elite-athlete culture site

The blog should consistently reinforce the SmarterTraining positioning:

> “Training for people with real lives.”

Every article should feel useful, grounded, trustworthy, and experience-informed.

---

# Core Audience

Write primarily for:
- busy professionals
- parents
- masters athletes
- indoor cyclists
- amateur endurance athletes
- people balancing training with work/family/life
- athletes with inconsistent schedules
- cyclists training 3–8 hours/week, not 20+

The reader is usually:
- motivated but constrained
- trying to stay fit sustainably
- frustrated by rigid plans
- not trying to become a WorldTour rider
- intelligent and experienced enough to dislike fluff

Assume the reader has some training knowledge already.

Do not write like you are explaining cycling to complete beginners unless the article explicitly targets beginners.

---

# Brand Voice

The tone should feel:

- practical
- intelligent
- calm
- slightly opinionated
- empathetic
- experience-driven
- grounded in reality
- quietly confident

The writing should sound like:
> an experienced athlete and coach who also understands work stress, family obligations, fatigue, and imperfect schedules.

Avoid:
- hyper-aggressive coaching language
- “grindset” culture
- hustle culture
- motivational yelling
- bro-science
- guru tone
- corporate marketing tone
- exaggerated enthusiasm
- robotic SEO phrasing

---

# Writing Style

## Preferred style

Use:
- concise paragraphs
- direct language
- descriptive headings
- concrete examples
- practical framing
- conversational clarity
- moderate sentence variety

Prefer:
- “Most amateur cyclists…”
- “In practice…”
- “For busy athletes…”
- “What usually works better…”
- “The real issue is often…”

Avoid:
- filler
- vague abstractions
- motivational clichés
- generic transitions
- empty summaries

---

# Banned / Discouraged Phrases

Never use:
- “In today’s fast-paced world”
- “Unlock your potential”
- “Game changer”
- “Take your training to the next level”
- “Crush your goals”
- “Optimize every aspect”
- “Studies show” (unless actual citations are present)
- “Science-backed” without specifics
- “Hack”
- “Biohack”
- “Elite performance secrets”
- “No excuses”
- “Train harder”
- “Maximum gains”
- “Dominate”
- “Revolutionary AI”

Avoid sounding like:
- affiliate SEO blogs
- LinkedIn thought leadership
- fitness influencers
- generic ChatGPT prose

---

# SEO Philosophy

The goal is:
> genuinely useful articles that also rank.

Do not write around keywords mechanically.

Each article should:
- target one primary search intent
- answer the question clearly
- provide practical insight
- include useful internal links
- use headings naturally
- avoid repetition

Do NOT:
- keyword stuff
- repeat phrases unnaturally
- write giant intros before answering the question
- create artificially long articles
- optimize for word count over usefulness

A shorter useful article is better than a long generic one.

---

# LLM Optimization Philosophy

Articles should be easy for both humans and AI systems to parse.

Every article should:
- answer the main question near the top
- define important concepts clearly
- use descriptive H2s
- include concise takeaway sections
- contain practical guidance
- avoid ambiguity and fluff

Assume portions of the article may be extracted into:
- AI search summaries
- snippets
- voice assistants
- Reddit discussions
- answer engines

Write so excerpts still make sense independently.

---

# Article Structure Standards

Default article structure:

1. H1 title
2. Short problem-oriented intro
3. Direct answer / key takeaway section
4. Main explanatory sections
5. Practical guidance
6. “How SmarterTraining thinks about this”
7. Related reading/internal links
8. FAQ section when appropriate
9. Soft CTA

Not every article needs every section, but this is the default structure.

---

# Heading Standards

Headings should:
- describe actual content
- be specific
- read naturally
- help scanning

Good:
- “Why Zone 2 can still create fatigue”
- “What busy cyclists usually get wrong”
- “How recovery changes after 35”

Bad:
- “Understanding the science”
- “Important considerations”
- “Maximizing results”
- “Final thoughts”

Prefer clarity over cleverness.

---

# Paragraph & Formatting Standards

Prefer:
- short-to-medium paragraphs
- occasional bullets
- occasional numbered lists
- strong readability

Avoid:
- giant walls of text
- excessive bullet spam
- over-formatting
- excessive bolding

Use bold sparingly for emphasis.

---

# SmarterTraining Mentions

The blog should not feel like constant product marketing.

SmarterTraining mentions should feel:
- contextual
- helpful
- understated
- natural

Good:
> “This is one reason SmarterTraining adapts recommendations based on recent fatigue and schedule context.”

Bad:
> “SmarterTraining solves this perfectly with revolutionary AI coaching.”

The product should appear as:
- thoughtful
- adaptive
- practical
- realistic

Never oversell.

---

# Training Philosophy Standards

The blog should consistently reinforce:

- consistency > perfection
- recovery matters
- sustainability matters
- flexibility matters
- training should fit life
- more is not always better
- amateur athletes have different constraints than pros
- missed workouts are normal
- adaptation matters more than rigid adherence

Avoid:
- perfectionism
- absolutism
- guilt-based framing

---

# Medical & Safety Standards

Never:
- diagnose injuries or medical conditions
- provide medical advice
- overstate physiological certainty
- recommend unsafe training behavior
- imply the app replaces coaches/doctors

Use caveats where appropriate:
- illness
- injury
- unusual fatigue
- dizziness
- persistent pain
- health concerns

Prefer:
> “If fatigue feels unusually severe or persistent, it may be worth backing off training and speaking with a medical professional.”

Avoid:
> “You are probably overtrained.”

---

# Comparison Article Standards

Comparison pages should be:
- fair
- nuanced
- useful

Do not:
- attack competitors
- use fake objectivity
- overstate differences

Acknowledge where competitors are good.

The differentiator should consistently come back to:
- adaptive flexibility
- real-life constraints
- recovery-aware recommendations
- sustainability
- time-crunched athletes

---

# Internal Linking Philosophy

Internal links should:
- genuinely help the reader
- connect related concepts
- reinforce category hubs
- improve discoverability naturally

Avoid:
- excessive linking
- generic “read more” links
- forcing links into irrelevant paragraphs

Prefer contextual links inside meaningful discussion.

---

# CTA Standards

CTAs should be soft and credible.

Good CTA tone:
- calm
- helpful
- invitational

Good examples:
- “SmarterTraining is built around this kind of adaptive decision-making.”
- “If your schedule changes often, adaptive recommendations can help reduce the guesswork.”
- “SmarterTraining was designed for athletes balancing training with real life.”

Avoid:
- hard sells
- urgency tricks
- hype
- exaggerated claims

---

# Editorial Quality Bar

Every article should feel:
- genuinely helpful
- experience-informed
- trustworthy
- human
- grounded
- practical
- internally consistent

If an article sounds like generic AI content, rewrite it.

If an article sounds like an SEO agency wrote it, rewrite it.

If an article sounds like a motivational influencer wrote it, rewrite it.

The goal is:
> the most thoughtful and useful training content for normal people trying to train seriously within real-life constraints.
