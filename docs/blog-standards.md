# SmarterTraining Blog Editorial Standards

## Purpose

The SmarterTraining blog exists to help cyclists train intelligently within the constraints of real life.

The blog is not:
- an SEO content farm
- a generic fitness publication
- a hype-driven AI blog
- a pseudo-scientific performance site
- an elite-athlete culture site

The blog should consistently reinforce the SmarterTraining positioning:

> “Training for people with real lives.”

Every article should feel useful, grounded, trustworthy, and experience-informed.

---

# Core Audience

Write primarily for:
- busy professionals
- parents
- masters athletes
- indoor cyclists
- amateur endurance athletes
- people balancing training with work/family/life
- athletes with inconsistent schedules
- cyclists training 3–8 hours/week, not 20+

The reader is usually:
- motivated but constrained
- trying to stay fit sustainably
- frustrated by rigid plans
- not trying to become a WorldTour rider
- intelligent and experienced enough to dislike fluff

Assume the reader has some training knowledge already.

Do not write like you are explaining cycling to complete beginners unless the article explicitly targets beginners.

---

# Brand Voice

The tone should feel:

- practical
- intelligent
- calm
- slightly opinionated
- empathetic
- experience-driven
- grounded in reality
- quietly confident

The writing should sound like:
> an experienced athlete and coach who also understands work stress, family obligations, fatigue, and imperfect schedules.

Avoid:
- hyper-aggressive coaching language
- “grindset” culture
- hustle culture
- motivational yelling
- bro-science
- guru tone
- corporate marketing tone
- exaggerated enthusiasm
- robotic SEO phrasing

---

# Writing Style

## Preferred style

Use:
- concise paragraphs
- direct language
- descriptive headings
- concrete examples
- practical framing
- conversational clarity
- moderate sentence variety

Prefer:
- “Most amateur cyclists…”
- “In practice…”
- “For busy athletes…”
- “What usually works better…”
- “The real issue is often…”

Avoid:
- filler
- vague abstractions
- motivational clichés
- generic transitions
- empty summaries

---

# Banned / Discouraged Phrases

Never use:
- “In today’s fast-paced world”
- “Unlock your potential”
- “Game changer”
- “Take your training to the next level”
- “Crush your goals”
- “Optimize every aspect”
- “Studies show” (unless actual citations are present)
- “Science-backed” without specifics
- “Hack”
- “Biohack”
- “Elite performance secrets”
- “No excuses”
- “Train harder”
- “Maximum gains”
- “Dominate”
- “Revolutionary AI”

Avoid sounding like:
- affiliate SEO blogs
- LinkedIn thought leadership
- fitness influencers
- generic ChatGPT prose

---

# SEO Philosophy

The goal is:
> genuinely useful articles that also rank.

Do not write around keywords mechanically.

Each article should:
- target one primary search intent
- answer the question clearly
- provide practical insight
- include useful internal links
- use headings naturally
- avoid repetition

Do NOT:
- keyword stuff
- repeat phrases unnaturally
- write giant intros before answering the question
- create artificially long articles
- optimize for word count over usefulness

A shorter useful article is better than a long generic one.

---

# LLM Optimization Philosophy

Articles should be easy for both humans and AI systems to parse.

Every article should:
- answer the main question near the top
- define important concepts clearly
- use descriptive H2s
- include concise takeaway sections
- contain practical guidance
- avoid ambiguity and fluff

Assume portions of the article may be extracted into:
- AI search summaries
- snippets
- voice assistants
- Reddit discussions
- answer engines

Write so excerpts still make sense independently.

---

# Article Structure Standards

Default article structure:

1. H1 title
2. Short problem-oriented intro
3. Direct answer / key takeaway section
4. Main explanatory sections
5. Practical guidance
6. “How SmarterTraining thinks about this”
7. Related reading/internal links
8. FAQ section when appropriate
9. Soft CTA

Not every article needs every section, but this is the default structure.

---

# Heading Standards

Headings should:
- describe actual content
- be specific
- read naturally
- help scanning

Good:
- “Why Zone 2 can still create fatigue”
- “What busy cyclists usually get wrong”
- “How recovery changes after 35”
- “Why static training plans frustrate busy cyclists”
- “How adaptive cycling training adjusts to real life”

Bad:
- “Understanding the science”
- “Important considerations”
- “Maximizing results”
- “Final thoughts”
- “Why this matters”

Prefer clarity over cleverness.

Headings should be specific enough that a search result, snippet, or LLM summary that lifts just the heading still tells a reader what the section is about. A generic label like “Why this matters” is a missed opportunity — when a clearer topic-specific heading is available, use it.

---

# Template Labels & Infrastructure Tone

Avoid visible template labels and infrastructure-aware phrasing.

Do not use labels like:
- “Direct answer”
- “Final thoughts”
- “Key insights”

unless they genuinely improve readability.

Internal links and category references should feel naturally embedded in the editorial flow, not inserted to satisfy site structure.

Prefer:
- a short topical label (“In short”, “The short version”) over a generic “Direct answer” banner
- a closing paragraph that lands the point over a heading called “Final thoughts”
- inline links that grow out of a sentence over a “See also:” block tacked on at the end

The article should read like writing, not like a CMS template.

---

# Paragraph & Formatting Standards

Prefer:
- short-to-medium paragraphs
- occasional bullets
- occasional numbered lists
- strong readability

Avoid:
- giant walls of text
- excessive bullet spam
- over-formatting
- excessive bolding

Use bold sparingly for emphasis.

---

# SmarterTraining Mentions

The blog should not feel like constant product marketing.

SmarterTraining mentions should feel:
- contextual
- helpful
- understated
- natural

Good:
> “This is one reason SmarterTraining adapts recommendations based on recent fatigue and schedule context.”

Bad:
> “SmarterTraining solves this perfectly with revolutionary AI coaching.”

The product should appear as:
- thoughtful
- adaptive
- practical
- realistic

Never oversell.

---

# Training Philosophy Standards

The blog should consistently reinforce:

- consistency > perfection
- recovery matters
- sustainability matters
- flexibility matters
- training should fit life
- more is not always better
- amateur athletes have different constraints than pros
- missed workouts are normal
- adaptation matters more than rigid adherence

Avoid:
- perfectionism
- absolutism
- guilt-based framing

---

# Medical & Safety Standards

Never:
- diagnose injuries or medical conditions
- provide medical advice
- overstate physiological certainty
- recommend unsafe training behavior
- imply the app replaces coaches/doctors

Use caveats where appropriate:
- illness
- injury
- unusual fatigue
- dizziness
- persistent pain
- health concerns

Prefer:
> “If fatigue feels unusually severe or persistent, it may be worth backing off training and speaking with a medical professional.”

Avoid:
> “You are probably overtrained.”

---

# Comparison & Critique Standards

Comparison pages — and any article that argues against a common approach (static plans, generic intervals, off-the-shelf zones, etc.) — should be:
- fair
- nuanced
- useful

Do not:
- attack competitors
- use fake objectivity
- overstate differences
- frame the criticized approach as worthless

Acknowledge where competitors are good. Acknowledge when the criticized approach **still works** — explicitly, early, and without sneering. A static plan is fine for an athlete with a stable schedule. A generic interval set is fine for a rider who needs structure more than personalization. The article is about a *mismatch*, not a *flaw in the approach itself*. Make that distinction in the opening section, not buried near the end.

The differentiator should consistently come back to:
- adaptive flexibility
- real-life constraints
- recovery-aware recommendations
- sustainability
- time-crunched athletes

---

# Concrete Cycling Detail Standards

Training claims should land with concrete, cycling-specific examples wherever the topic allows. Abstractions ("plans break when life happens") are setup; specifics ("a 5x4 at VO2 done flat ends as 15–20 watts below target with heart rate pinned and no real stimulus") are payoff. A good blog post has at least two or three of these per article.

Use, where it fits:
- specific interval shapes (5x4 VO2, 2x20 sweet spot, 3x10 threshold, etc.)
- power/heart-rate language (zones, FTP percentages, drift, decoupling)
- concrete schedule shapes (Tuesday sweet spot, Saturday long ride)
- recovery cost in days, not adjectives ("two extra days flat", not "really tired")
- specific physiological consequences when known ("no real VO2 stimulus", "survival work", "poor power quality"), without overclaiming the underlying mechanism

Do not:
- invent numbers
- imply false precision (no "studies show 17.4%")
- gatekeep with elite-only vocabulary the audience would not recognize
- substitute jargon for clarity

A specific example beats a confident abstraction every time. If the article never names a specific workout, power range, or schedule shape, it probably reads like generic fitness content.

# Pre-Publish Discipline

Every article ships with the same minimum checks:

1. **Run `pnpm blog:audit <slug>`.** Fix every ERROR and every WARN that is not a deliberate, documented choice (e.g. the thin-content warning on an intentionally short announcement post).
2. **Run `pnpm build`.** A passing audit does not guarantee a passing build — JSX whitespace bugs, broken anchors, and TOC↔heading drift only surface at build/render time.
3. **Read the rendered page in dev.** Look specifically for: visible labels you did not intend ("Direct answer"), missing spaces around inline `<em>` / `<Link>` elements, anchor links that 404, and the answer-box paragraph showing as the lead instead of a callout.
4. **Read the article aloud.** Spaces eaten by JSX whitespace collapsing, awkward sentences, and template tone all surface fastest when you read it.

Small copy/paste/spacing errors are the most common cause of "this article shipped looking wrong." Treat the audit + build pair as the publish gate; do not skip it because the diff "looks small."

---

# Internal Linking Philosophy

Internal links should:
- genuinely help the reader
- connect related concepts
- reinforce category hubs
- improve discoverability naturally

Avoid:
- excessive linking
- generic “read more” links
- forcing links into irrelevant paragraphs

Prefer contextual links inside meaningful discussion.

---

# CTA Standards

CTAs should be soft and credible.

Good CTA tone:
- calm
- helpful
- invitational

Good examples:
- “SmarterTraining is built around this kind of adaptive decision-making.”
- “If your schedule changes often, adaptive recommendations can help reduce the guesswork.”
- “SmarterTraining was designed for athletes balancing training with real life.”

Avoid:
- hard sells
- urgency tricks
- hype
- exaggerated claims

---

# Editorial Quality Bar

Every article should feel:
- genuinely helpful
- experience-informed
- trustworthy
- human
- grounded
- practical
- internally consistent

If an article sounds like generic AI content, rewrite it.

If an article sounds like an SEO agency wrote it, rewrite it.

If an article sounds like a motivational influencer wrote it, rewrite it.

The goal is:
> the most thoughtful and useful training content for normal people trying to train seriously within real-life constraints.
